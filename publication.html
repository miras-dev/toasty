From Malabar to Berlin: Our Flavour Journey

Growing up along the Malabar coast shaped everything about how we think about food. The coast of Kerala is alive with spice — cardamom drying on flat rooftops, the smell of fresh coconut milk from the market early in the morning, fish curries simmering in clay pots that have been used for generations. Food there is not entertainment. It is memory. It is care. It is the language your grandmother speaks when she has no other words.

When the four of us ended up in Berlin — through different routes, at different times — we found something unexpected: a city that was deeply curious about the world. Open to new things. Hungry in every sense of the word. But for us, there was a gap. The food we craved when we were homesick wasn't available. Not the way we knew it. Not made with the care we grew up with.

The food we brought with us was never meant to be exotic. It was just what home tasted like. And home, we learned, can be anywhere you make it.

We started cooking for ourselves. Then for friends. Then one Sunday, we turned the living room into a pop-up and fed thirty people a Kerala-style breakfast: appam, egg curry, banana chips, spiced chai. Every single person asked when we were doing it again.

That question was the beginning of Toasty Turtle.

The name came later — but the turtle metaphor fits so well that it feels like it was always there. In Kerala, the turtle is ancient, patient, and consistent. It moves at its own pace and it always arrives. That's exactly the energy we wanted to bring to Berlin. No rush. No shortcuts. Good food made with intention.

Our menu is not a traditional Kerala menu. It is something else — a conversation between the Malabar coast and the produce we find in Berlin's markets. The spiced chai we serve uses the same ratio my grandmother taught me, but we steep it in oat milk because that's what many of our guests need. The Kerala curry is as close to home as we can make it here. The avocado toast carries a subtle coconut-chilli oil that makes it ours.

Every dish carries the question: what does this taste like when it's made slowly, made with love, made for someone you want to make happy?

That's the journey. Malabar to Berlin. Every single day.
